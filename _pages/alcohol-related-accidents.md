## Your Related Losses

If you are injured in a car accident that was caused by a drunk driver, you can face very serious losses that include all the following:


- Property damage to your car and its contents
- Medical bills, which are likely to be ongoing
- Lost income, which may need to address lost earning power into the future
- Physical and emotional pain and suffering, which can be immense and should not be downplayed

An essential aspect of your drunk driving claim is outlining the full scope of your covered losses, and you can count on your persuasive drunk driving accident lawyer to make this happen.

## Proving the Other Driver’s Impairment

To recover compensation for your complete losses, you’ll have to prove the at-fault driver’s negligence, which in this case is impairment. This generally comes down to evidence like the following:


- The testimony of eyewitnesses, such as if they saw the other motorist driving erratically
- Photos and videos captured by dashcams, security cameras, or bystanders
- Physical evidence at the scene
- Your own account of how the accident occurred

The police report will also be carefully considered, and if the attending officer issued a DUI citation to the other driver, it can go a long way toward supporting your claim. If you have reason to believe that the other driver was impaired at the time of the accident, sharing this information with the officer at the scene is advised.

## The Sobering Statistics

The National Highway and Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) shares important [drunk driving statistics](https://www.nhtsa.gov/risky-driving/drunk-driving){:target="_blank" rel="noopener"}, which include that approximately 37 people die each day in drunk driving accidents that occur across the nation. In a recent year alone, this amounted to more than 13,500 [deaths](/personal-injury-overview/wrongful-death/) – all of which were completely preventable. Alcohol is a relevant variable in a full 32 percent of all fatal traffic accidents in this country, which means that nearly a third of all deadly crashes involve alcohol.

The consumption of alcohol leads to impairments in relation to thinking, reasoning, and coordination, and each of these functions is critical to driving safely. While the fact that impaired driving is dangerous is well known and well understood, too many motorists refuse to stop drinking and driving, and our roadways are far more dangerous as a result.
